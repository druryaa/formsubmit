ENFORCING COURT ORDERS

Contempt of Court

CONTEMPT

WHAT IS CONTEMPT?

When a Court makes an order, it is meant to be followed. At times, some litigants refuse to follow orders. That refusal may be because they misunderstand the order, they are unable to comply with the order, or they simply do not want to follow the order.

In the event an order is not be complied with, a party may ask the Court to find the party failing to abide by an order, in "contempt" of Court. When a party is found in contempt of Court, there are a number of remedies the Court may order including:

• Fine the person for failing to comply with the order. These fines can be paid to the party who brought the motion.

• Order a term of imprisonment.

• Order that certain terms be complied with in a given time period failing which a monetary penalty will be imposed.

• Remove a party from participating in the litigation process.

Due to the severity of a finding of contempt, it is a remedy of last resort. More importantly, it is treated as a criminal proceeding, due to the severity of the finding. It is for that reason, litigants are strongly encouraged to comply with Court orders. In the event an order cannot be complied with, a motion ought to be brought to vary an order that is impossible to satisfy.

Recently, our client was successful in having the responding party found in contempt of Court for failing to abide by a non-depletion order, in Hevey v. Hevey 2022 ONSC 6685.
