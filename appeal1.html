APPEAL WORK

Appealing a Court Ruling

APPEALING ORDERS

Appeals require parties to follow a strict timeline for the issuance of a notice of appeal. Depending on the type of order under appeal, the timeline for the issuance of a notice of appeal can be short.

f you are not satisfied with the terms of an order, it is imperative that you contact a lawyer experienced with appellate work immediately to understand your options.

Leave to Appeal

orders require leave to appeal. Leave to appeal is the Court granting permission to appeal the order. A leave to appeal request is a motion, that is brought in the Court that you are seeking to appeal the order. Certain orders, for example a finding of contempt, do not require leave to appeal, and the party seeking to have the decision reviewed by an appellate Court is permitted to proceed with their appeal.

However, some orders, for example temporary disclosure orders, require the Appellate Court to grant leave to appeal. In order to be granted leave to appeal this type of order, typically the Court must be satisfied that (1) there is a conflicting decision by another judge or Court involved in the proposed appeal and it is, in the opinion of the judge hearing the motion, desirable that leave to appeal be granted; or (2) it appears to the judge hearing the motion for leave to appeal that there is good reason to doubt the correctness of the order in question and the proposed appeal involves matters of such importance that, in his or her opinion, leave to appeal should be granted.

Leave Granted or Leave Not Required

Typically, after leave is granted, or if no leave to appeal is required, the moving party on the appeal “perfects” their appeal in the timeframe prescribed by the legislation. Perfecting an appeal requires providing the Appellate Court with all the necessary documentation to hear the appeal, this typically includes the materials that were before the Court on the matter under appeal together with a certified copy of the transcript of hearing.

After the appeal has been perfected, the responding party is given an opportunity to file their responding materials for the Appellate Court to consider.

Appeals are usually heard by a panel of three Justices, who have reviewed the materials filed ahead of the hearing. During the actual hearing of the appeal, the Justices are given an opportunity to probe issues they may have with the appeal, and challenge arguments that may cause issues for the panel.
